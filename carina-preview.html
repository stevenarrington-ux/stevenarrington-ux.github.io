Overview

Summary

Cvent's web applications were built by dozens of independent teams, each solving similar problems differently. As the company prepared for major growth — and eventually a pandemic requiring rapid pivots — we needed a unified foundation that could scale.

As Principal Designer, I led the team that built Carina, our design system. Named after both the constellation and the keel of a boat, it set our course into the future.

Team: Me (system lead), 1 product manager, 2 senior product designers, 1 visual designer, 4 developers, 11 ambassadors

Users: ~500 developers across 75 teams, 50 designers, 30,000+ Cvent end users

My Role
System Design & Architecture Governance & DesignOps Documentation Continuous Education Cross-team Coordination

"From pattern creation, review, and design library implementation, Steve always made design a conversation. Always aware, balancing, and encouraging creative freedom in a project that naturally restricts it."

The Challenge

A System Nobody Trusted

  • Five files, zero truth — A designer searched for a component and found 5 different published versions. Nobody knew which was current.
  • Updates that broke things — Designers had been conditioned to never accept library updates. Accepting meant hours of cleanup. Trust was gone.
  • Faster without the system — When it's quicker to build from scratch than use your design system, you don't have a design system. You have a liability.
  • Influence, not authority — 50 designers across 75 teams, each with their own PMs and priorities. I couldn't mandate adoption. I had to earn it.
How I Led This

Building the Foundation

Comprehensive Documentation

Each component required considerations for usability, responsiveness, accessibility, and localization. I created templates to ensure accurate and standard documentation across 40+ Confluence pages and an internal resource site.

Each component was meticulously designed and documented. Designer and dev feedback through Slack and office hours helped us iterate.

Token Definition

I started with tokens, not components. Color, spacing, typography, and animation tokens formed the base layer — the part that enforces brand consistency without constraining how teams compose screens.

Token definition

Component Design

Every component went through a structured review: I demonstrated how it worked, walked through variants and the Properties panel, then designers compared old vs. new and voted on naming. I stress-tested every instance outside the library to prevent the update anxiety that had killed trust in previous systems.

Each component had light, dark, and low-fi versions. (Yes, buttons were this complex.)

Governance Processes

I worked with the team to define processes for contribution, enhancement requests, and component standards. We encouraged creative use and established clear paths for teams to contribute back.

We actively encouraged devs and designers to think of creative ways to use the design system. When they did, we established ways for them to contribute back.

Built for Theming

I defined tokens for color, typography, and animations. The dev team built it so users could provide primary and secondary colors — or just point to their corporate website — and the palette would apply to their event site or branded Cvent UI.

Theming in action (light, dark, and wireframe)

Scaling Through People

The Ambassador Program

When we migrated from Sketch to Figma, I created a program where designers throughout Cvent could contribute to polishing components. At one point, I coordinated over 11 designers, plus copywriters and developers, dividing work on 90 components over 3 months. This made adoption, contribution, and appreciation for the system stronger.

Making Education Continuous

I created training videos, led design org presentations, held office hours, and detailed releases in documentation and Slack. For new patterns, I reached out to the entire design organization to contribute ideas, running collaborative workshops to gather input.

Workshopping the pattern for Insights

Measuring Success

Carina vs. the Visual Refresh

Following Carina's launch, I surveyed users to see how well we aligned with our design goals. Carina scored significantly higher than our previous "Visual Refresh" on multiple brand values — particularly on feeling modern and innovative while maintaining professionalism.

When It Mattered Most

The COVID Test

In Spring 2020, Cvent lost its revenue source overnight. In-person events disappeared. Product Management decided to add a virtual conference option. Using Carina components, we launched in one month.

⚡

Within one year, the virtual conference product earned over $100M. The design system's maturity made this speed possible.

What I Designed

I led design for our most commonly used patterns. Each included interactive states, flows, use cases, and dos and don'ts. Each had light, dark, and low-fidelity wireframe versions.

What I Learned
  • Scale through people, not just components — The Ambassador Program created ownership across the organization. When people contribute, they advocate.
  • Education is continuous, not a launch event — Training videos, office hours, Slack channels, workshops — multiple formats for different learning styles.
  • Document everything, even the obvious — What seems obvious to the creator is not obvious to the 75th team adopting the system.
  • Build for the crisis you don't see coming — We couldn't have launched virtual conferences in one month without the foundation we'd already built.
